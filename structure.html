How Nexus is organised

The full picture.

Everything here is the real structure — how roles work, who does what, how groups form, how safety is handled, and what the long-term vision is.

The structure

Three layers. One platform.

Nexus runs on Discord. The server is divided into three levels, each with a different function and a different kind of leadership.

Layer 1 — The Platform
⚡ Nexus
The whole server. One shared Discord space that everyone is part of. Server-wide announcements, community guidelines, and the admin team live here. This is the home base for everything below it.
↓
Layer 2 — Interest Domains
🗂️ Domains
Broad interest areas — Social Discussion, Art, Environment, Sports, Startups, Books, Board Games, Outdoors, Running, Anime, and more. Each domain has its own channel and a Domain Leader. Domains are the organising layer between the whole platform and individual groups.
↓
Layer 3 — Discussion Groups
💬 Groups
Small, focused groups inside each domain. These are where the real conversations happen — on a specific topic, interest, or activity. Groups are application-based and kept deliberately small. Each group has a student Group Leader who manages membership and shapes the culture. Groups can and should meet offline — that's the point.
Roles

Who does what.

Every role in Nexus is held by a student. There are no teachers, no school administrators, no external organisations with authority inside the platform.

⚡
Admin Team
Platform-level · appointed
The admin team runs the platform as a whole. They are not members of any single domain — they sit above the domain structure and oversee everything.
Maintain the server — channels, permissions, bot settings
Approve new members after reviewing their expression of interest
Handle safety incidents and escalate serious cases to the eSafety Commissioner if needed
Connect Domain Leaders with external organisations when requested
Run monthly community check-ins to keep the platform healthy
Facilitate the first offline events in the early stage
🏅
Domain Leader
Domain-level · appointed by admin
Each Domain Leader oversees everything within their interest area. They are the link between individual groups and the admin team.
Post weekly updates or discussion prompts in their domain channel
Meet (casually) with Group Leaders inside their domain at least once a month
Receive requests from Group Leaders for external collaboration and pass them to admin
Flag inactive groups or members to admin
Help new members find the right group
👑
Group Leader
Group-level · self-proposed or appointed
Group Leaders run a specific discussion group. They set the direction, accept or decline membership requests, and are responsible for the quality of the group.
Define the group's focus and keep it on track
Review and accept membership requests (short written message required)
Propose and organise offline meetups for the group
Post at least once per week to keep the group active
Report issues to their Domain Leader
✅
Member
Group-level · application-based
Regular members are the core of Nexus. Getting in isn't automatic — you apply to a specific group with a short message about why you want to join. This keeps groups intentional and high-quality.
Participate actively in at least one group
Show up to offline meetups when possible
Respect community guidelines at all times
Report anything that feels unsafe or wrong via the report channel
Can propose a new group or domain at any time
How groups form

Groups are proposed, not assigned.

Anyone can propose a group. No one is placed in one automatically. The process is designed to make sure groups form around genuine interest — not random assignment.

1

Anyone proposes a group

A member (or prospective member) has an idea for a group — a topic, activity, or interest. They post the idea in the relevant domain channel. There's no form. Just write it.

2

Domain Leader reviews it

The Domain Leader checks whether the idea fits the domain and whether there's enough interest. If yes, they pass it to admin to set up the channel.

3

Group is created and a leader is appointed

Admin creates the group channel. The person who proposed it usually becomes the Group Leader — unless someone else is better suited. The leader sets the group's focus and rules.

4

Members apply to join

Anyone interested sends a short message to the Group Leader — a few sentences about why they want to join. The leader reads it and decides. No one is added automatically.

✓

The group starts meeting

Online first — Discord. Offline as soon as it's feasible. A café, a park, a library, wherever makes sense. Offline activity is actively encouraged, never required.

External connections

How Nexus connects to the outside world.

One of Nexus's long-term goals is to connect student groups with external organisations — NGOs, universities, companies, community groups. Here's exactly how that works, and who controls it.

1

Group Leader makes a request

A Group Leader wants to connect with an external organisation — e.g. an environmental NGO, a university research team, a local business. They write a short proposal: what the group is, what they want to do together, and why.

2

Domain Leader reviews and passes to admin

The Domain Leader checks the proposal makes sense for the domain and that the group is active enough to follow through. Then it goes to admin.

3

Admin makes the connection

Admin contacts the external organisation. This is the only point where someone outside Nexus is involved. Admin introduces the platform, explains what the group wants to do, and facilitates the initial contact. The group then takes it from there.

Important: External organisations do not have access to the Discord server. They do not moderate, observe, or participate in internal discussions. The only contact they have is with the admin team and, after introduction, with the specific group that requested the connection.

Safety

How safety is handled.

Nexus is an independent student community. There is no school involvement. Safety is handled entirely by the student admin team, operating in line with Australian government online safety guidelines.

Entry

Everyone completes an expression of interest. Admin reviews each application. No one is admitted automatically. Anonymous accounts are not accepted.

Verification

New members confirm they are Australian high school students. Admin aims to conduct a short (15-minute) onboarding conversation with each new member before access is granted.

Discord setup

New members can only see welcome channels until they complete onboarding. Full access is granted by admin after the onboarding step is done.

Reporting

There is a dedicated #report-an-issue channel. Members can write there — only admin can read it. Reports are acknowledged within 24 hours.

Moderation

Admin monitors all public channels. Minor issues are handled by admin directly. Serious issues are escalated to the eSafety Commissioner's reporting system.

Offline meetings

Offline meetings are encouraged in public places — cafés, parks, libraries. Members are advised never to share personal addresses. The first meeting with someone new should always be in a public space.

Legal framework: Nexus operates in line with the Australian Online Safety Act 2021 and the eSafety Commissioner's guidelines for safe digital participation by young people. As an education and community support platform, Nexus falls outside the social media age restriction laws that apply to platforms like Instagram and TikTok. If you have safety concerns, you can contact the eSafety Commissioner at esafety.gov.au/report.

Community guidelines

The rules. Short version.

These apply everywhere — online and offline, in every group, in every domain.

01

Be respectful. Disagree with ideas, not people. Debate is welcome. Hostility is not.

02

No harassment, discrimination, or hate speech. Any content targeting someone's identity, background, or beliefs will result in immediate removal.

03

No sharing personal information. Don't share your address, phone number, or school timetable. Don't ask others for theirs. This applies online and offline.

04

Stay in your lane. If you're in the Chess group, don't spam the Skiing group. Keep conversations in the right channels.

05

No screenshots without consent. What happens in Nexus stays in Nexus unless the people involved agree otherwise.

06

Report, don't retaliate. If something feels wrong, use #report-an-issue. Admin handles it. Don't escalate it yourself.

07

Be present. If you join a group, show up. Groups exist because people chose to be in them. Don't join and disappear — it's not fair on the people who are active.

The long-term vision

Where this is going.

Nexus starts as one Discord server. But the structure is designed to scale — to multiple schools, multiple communities, and eventually real connections with organisations and institutions that can give students access to the wider world.

01

One school

Start with a single community. Build the culture, test the structure, prove the model.

02

Multiple schools

Add school chapters. Students from different schools share domains and cross-school groups form naturally around shared interests.

03

Society

Active groups connect with NGOs, universities, companies. Students encounter the real world while still at school — on their own terms.
